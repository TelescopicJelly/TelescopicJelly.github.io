Importance/History


Early botany

Early botany originated from herbalism, the study of plants for medicinal purposes. Ancient civilizations, including those in India, Egypt, Persia, and China, documented plant classifications. Theophrastus (c. 371–287 BCE), a Greek scholar and student of Aristotle, is regarded as the "Father of Botany" for his works Enquiry into Plants and On the Causes of Plants. Greek physician Pedanius Dioscorides wrote De materia medica in the 1st century, which remained influential for over 1,500 years. Medieval Muslim scholars, including Ibn Wahshiyya, Abū Ḥanīfa Dīnawarī, and Ibn al-Baitar, also made significant botanical contributions. In the 16th century, botanical gardens were established in Italian universities, advancing botany as an academic field. German physicians Leonhart Fuchs, Otto Brunfels, and Hieronymus Bock contributed to plant classification and original botanical studies. Valerius Cordus authored Historia Plantarum and a pharmacopoeia, while Ulisse Aldrovandi was a pioneer of natural history. In 1665, Robert Hooke used a microscope to discover and name "cells" in cork, marking a major breakthrough in plant science.
